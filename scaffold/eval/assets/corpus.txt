The repair shop occupied the back half of a building that had once been a print works, and the
smell of old ink still came up through the concrete on damp mornings. Mara liked it best then,
before the others arrived, when the only sound was the slow tick of the space heater and the
occasional complaint of a hard drive being asked to do something it had given up on years before.
She kept a row of salvaged machines along the north wall, each labelled with a strip of masking
tape and a name that meant something only to her: the one called Tuesday because it had arrived on
a Tuesday, the one called Argument because it had caused one. People brought her hardware that the
manufacturers had long since declared dead, and more often than not she coaxed another few years
out of it, not by magic but by patience and a refusal to accept that old and useless were the same
word.

There is a particular satisfaction in making a slow thing fast, or at least less slow. It is not
the satisfaction of building something new, which is loud and obvious, but a quieter pleasure, the
kind you get from finding the one loose screw that was making the whole cabinet rattle. The trick,
she would tell anyone who asked and several who did not, is to measure first and guess later. Most
people do it the other way around. They have a theory about why the machine is slow, and they spend
an afternoon acting on the theory, and at the end of the afternoon the machine is exactly as slow
as before but now they are also tired. Measure first. Find out where the time actually goes. The
machine will tell you, if you ask it in the right language and are willing to believe the answer
even when it is inconvenient.

Consider the simple question of how quickly a processor can read a long list of numbers from memory
and add them together. You might think the answer depends on how fast the processor can add, and
for a short list held close at hand that is true. But stretch the list out until it no longer fits
in the small fast memories near the core, and something changes. Now the processor spends most of
its time waiting for the numbers to arrive from the larger, slower memory further away. It could
add far faster than the numbers can be delivered, so its arithmetic talent sits idle, drumming its
fingers. The bottleneck has moved. No amount of additional arithmetic skill will help; the only
thing that helps is delivering the numbers faster, or needing fewer of them. This is the whole idea,
dressed in plain clothes: some tasks are limited by thinking, and some by fetching, and the wise
optimizer learns to tell which is which before reaching for a tool.

Weather has its own kind of memory. A cold snap in late autumn is remembered by the soil long after
the air has warmed again, and the first hard frost of the year sets a clock running in every
deciduous tree, a countdown toward the sudden synchronized letting-go that paints the hillsides and
clogs the gutters. The farmers in the valley read these signs the way a mechanic reads a vibration,
not as omens but as data, accumulated over seasons into something that looks from the outside like
intuition and is really just a very long memory put to practical use. Ask one of them how they knew
the rain was coming and they will shrug and say the light was wrong, and they will be unable to tell
you exactly what wrong means, because the knowledge lives below the level of words.

To make a loaf of bread you need flour, water, salt, and time, and of these the most important and
the most often rushed is time. Mix the flour and water and leave them alone for an hour before you
add anything else, and the flour will drink the water and begin, on its own, to organize itself into
the long elastic strands that give bread its structure. Skip this step and you can still make bread,
but you will have to do with force and kneading what the flour would happily have done by itself if
only you had been patient. There is a lesson in this that extends well beyond the kitchen, though
stated too directly it sounds like the sort of thing printed on a mug, so we will leave it implied
and return to the dough, which by now has doubled and is straining gently against the cloth.

The history of tools is in large part a history of moving work from the body to the object. The
first knife moved the work of tearing from the teeth to the hand. The lever moved the work of
lifting from the back to a length of timber and a well-chosen stone. Each step let a person do more
with less, and each step also quietly changed what kind of person was needed, so that the world of
the lever rewarded different talents than the world of the bare hand. Computers are the latest and
fastest-moving chapter of this old story, and they have a peculiar property the earlier tools
lacked: they can be improved without being rebuilt. A better idea about how to use the same machine
can arrive on Tuesday and make the machine twice as useful by Wednesday, with not a single screw
turned. This is why the people who write the instructions matter as much as the people who cast the
metal, and why a careful afternoon of measurement and thought can be worth more than a truckload of
new hardware.

Letters used to take weeks to cross an ocean, and people wrote them differently because of it. You
could not dash off a quick correction the next morning, so you thought before you wrote, and you
wrote as though the words would have to stand for a season. There is a school of thought that says
the slowness made the writing better, and another that says this is nostalgia dressed as principle,
and the truth is probably that it made the writing different in ways that were sometimes better and
sometimes merely slower. What is certain is that the medium shaped the message, as it always does.
The constraints of a tool are not separate from the work the tool produces; they are pressed into
it, like the grain of the wood into the surface of the table.

A note on counting, since so much of the work comes back to it. To count a thing honestly you must
first decide exactly what you are counting, and this is harder than it sounds. If you are timing how
long a task takes, do you start the clock when you ask for the task or when the task actually begins,
and do you stop it when the last useful result appears or when the machine finally goes quiet? The
two choices can differ by a wide margin, and a person who is not careful, or who wishes not to be,
can make almost any change look like an improvement simply by sliding the start and stop lines to
flattering positions. The defence against this is boring and total: write down the rules before you
run the test, run the test the same way every time, and report the spread of the results and not
only the single number you liked best. A measurement without its uncertainty is a rumour.

In the evening the shop filled with the particular tiredness of people who have been concentrating,
which is different from the tiredness of people who have been lifting. Mara would run one last test,
a long one, the kind that takes the better part of an hour and cannot be hurried, and while it ran
she would sit with a cup of tea gone lukewarm and watch the numbers crawl across the screen. Faster
than yesterday, usually. Not always. Some days the honest answer was that the change she had been so
sure about had made no difference at all, or had made things worse, and on those days she wrote the
result down exactly as it was, because a result you wish were different is still a result, and the
only thing worse than a slow machine is a fast lie about a slow machine. Then she turned off the
heater, and the slow tick stopped, and the building settled into the cold to wait for morning.

Salt does more than make food taste of itself. In the right amount it draws water out of vegetables
and firms them, slows the spoiling of meat, and tightens the web of proteins in a rising dough so it
can hold the gas that makes it light. Too little and the loaf is flat and dull; too much and the
yeast labours and the crust burns before the middle sets. The cook learns the amount not from a
table but from a thousand small corrections, each one a tiny experiment whose result is eaten before
it can be written down. Much skill is like this, an archive of forgotten experiments, and it is one
of the quiet advantages of writing things down that you can stop relying on an archive you cannot
search and start building one you can.

The river below the town had been straightened a century ago to make room for the railway, and for
a hundred years it behaved, running fast and shallow between its engineered banks. Then a wet decade
arrived, and the river remembered, in the way rivers do, that it had once meandered across the whole
floor of the valley, and it set about reclaiming its old course one flood at a time. The engineers
who had straightened it were long dead, and their certainty had died with them, and the people who
inherited the problem learned the expensive lesson that a system forced into a shape it does not want
will spend years patiently pushing back. The cheapest solution, in the end, was to give the river a
little of its old room and stop fighting the whole of its nature for the sake of a tidy line on a map.
